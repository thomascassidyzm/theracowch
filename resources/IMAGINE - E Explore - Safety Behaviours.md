## Understanding the Hidden Trap

---

### READ ‚Äì What Are Safety Behaviours?

Safety behaviours are the subtle things we do to prevent feared outcomes or manage anxiety. Unlike complete avoidance (not going to the party at all), safety behaviours let us do the thing... but with protective measures in place.

**Common examples:**
- Going to social events but staying glued to your best friend
- Making phone calls but having a script written out first
- Going to the shops but wearing headphones to avoid interaction
- Attending meetings but sitting near the exit
- Dating but constantly seeking reassurance you're "doing it right"
- Checking your phone repeatedly to make sure no one's upset with you
- Having a drink before social situations to "take the edge off"
- Over-preparing for everything to prevent failure

### The Paradox

Safety behaviours feel helpful because anxiety drops temporarily. But here's what's really happening:

**The Safety Behaviour Trap:**
1. You do the safety behaviour
2. The feared outcome doesn't happen
3. You conclude: *"I'm only safe BECAUSE I did that behaviour"*
4. The behaviour is reinforced
5. Anxiety remains (or grows stronger)
6. You become dependent on the safety behaviour

**The truth:** The feared outcome probably wouldn't have happened anyway, but you never get to learn that.

### Why They're Different from Helpful Coping

**Safety behaviour (unhelpful):** Checking your phone 50 times to make sure your partner isn't upset with you
**Healthy coping (helpful):** Having one honest conversation: "I sometimes worry you're upset - can we agree you'll tell me directly if something's wrong?"

**Safety behaviour (unhelpful):** Only going places with a "safe person" beside you
**Healthy coping (helpful):** Gradually building confidence in going places alone, starting small

**The key difference:** Healthy coping builds capability and confidence. Safety behaviours prevent learning and maintain dependency.

**Social situations:**

- Testing your partner repeatedly

- Excessive list-making/planning

## READ ‚Äì The Evidence That Sets You Free

The only way to discover you don't need a safety behaviour is to **test what happens without it**.

This is called **behavioural experiments** in CBT, and it's the gold standard for overcoming anxiety.

### The Scientific Process:

1. **Prediction:** "If I don't do my safety behaviour, X will happen"
2. **Experiment:** Reduce or drop the safety behaviour in a controlled way
3. **Observe:** What actually happened?
4. **Learn:** Update your understanding based on evidence, not fear

**Example:**

- **Prediction:** "If I don't check my phone every 10 minutes, my partner will get mad and I won't know until it's too late"
- **Experiment:** Check phone only 3 times during a 4-hour period
- **Observe:** Partner wasn't upset. Nothing bad happened. Felt anxious at first, then it settled.
- **Learn:** I can cope with uncertainty. My partner will tell me directly if something's wrong. My anxiety was lying to me.

## TRY ‚Äì The Approach Ladder

üïê 20 minutes to create, then ongoing practice

When you want to tackle a bigger fear, build an **approach ladder** - a series of gradually challenging steps that remove safety behaviours one at a time.

### How to Build Your Ladder:

**1. Define the Goal**

What situation do you currently avoid or do with heavy safety behaviours?

Example: "Give a presentation at work without hiding/reading from notes"

**2. Rate Your Current Anxiety**

If you had to do this RIGHT NOW with NO safety behaviours: ___/10

**3. Break It Into 10 Steps**

Start with something that would cause 2-3/10 anxiety, build up to 9-10/10.

**Each step should:**
- Remove one safety behaviour OR
- Add one small challenge
- Feel doable with some courage

### Example Ladder ‚Äì Social Confidence:

| Step | Challenge | Anxiety (predicted) |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| 1 | Say hello to shopkeeper (with friend nearby as safety behaviour) | 2/10 |
| 2 | Say hello to shopkeeper (alone - removing safety behaviour) | 3/10 |
| 3 | Ask shopkeeper a question about a product | 4/10 |
| 4 | Make small talk with stranger while waiting (friend nearby) | 5/10 |
| 5 | Make small talk with stranger (alone) | 6/10 |
| 6 | Attend small social gathering (30 mins only - time limit as safety behaviour) | 7/10 |
| 7 | Attend small gathering (stay for 1 hour) | 7/10 |
| 8 | Attend gathering and talk to someone new | 8/10 |
| 9 | Attend party (no time limit, no exit strategy planned) | 9/10 |
| 10 | Host a small gathering yourself | 9/10 |

### Rules for Climbing:

1. **Start at Step 1** (not where you "should" be able to start)
2. **Repeat each step until anxiety drops by 50%** (might take 3-5 practices)
3. **Only move up when ready** (if step feels too big, add a middle step)
4. **Expect anxiety** (that's how your brain learns!)
5. **Celebrate every step** (you're rewiring your brain with evidence)

## READ ‚Äì Common Fears About Dropping Safety Behaviours

### "But what if the bad thing DOES happen?"

Then you'll handle it. And you'll have evidence that you can cope. That's actually better than living in constant fear of maybe-possibly-it-might-happen.

Remember: Safety behaviours don't actually prevent bad things from happening. They just make you FEEL like you're preventing them.

### "What if my anxiety gets too high?"

Anxiety is uncomfortable but not dangerous. And here's the science: if you stay in the situation without the safety behaviour, anxiety WILL peak and then naturally come down. That's how you learn.

If you keep escaping or using safety behaviours, you never learn that anxiety drops on its own.

### "Some things ARE risky - shouldn't I be careful?"

Yes! The difference:

**Reasonable precaution:** Wearing a seatbelt, looking both ways before crossing, preparing for an important presentation

**Safety behaviour:** Checking your seatbelt 15 times, avoiding all roads, spending 20 hours preparing for a 5-minute presentation

Ask yourself: "Is this proportionate to the actual risk?" and "Does everyone else do this much?"

### "What if I fail without my safety behaviour?"

Maybe you will. And maybe you'll learn that failure isn't catastrophic. You'll survive it, learn from it, and try again.

Right now, your safety behaviour is preventing you from learning what you're actually capable of.

## GROW ‚Äì Self-Compassion for Safety Behaviours

Remember: You developed these behaviours for good reasons. They were your brain's attempt to keep you safe.

**Compassionate self-talk:**
- "These behaviours made sense based on what I feared"
- "I'm ready to learn I can cope without them"
- "It's OK to feel anxious while I practice"
- "Every small step is rewiring my brain"
- "I'm building the courage muscle"

**What NOT to say:**
- "I'm so stupid for doing this"
- "I should be over this by now"
- "Everyone else can do this easily"
- "What's wrong with me?"

Change happens through curiosity and compassion, not criticism.

## READ ‚Äì The Science of Unlearning

When you repeatedly face situations without safety behaviours and nothing bad happens, your brain learns through a process called **inhibitory learning**.

Your brain doesn't erase the fear memory, but it creates a new, stronger memory: "I did the thing without protection and I was OK."

Each time you practice:
- ‚úÖ Strengthens the "I can cope" pathway
- ‚úÖ Weakens the "I must do this to be safe" pathway
- ‚úÖ Increases your window of tolerance for uncertainty
- ‚úÖ Builds genuine confidence (not false safety)

This is why repeated practice is essential. One good experience isn't enough - you need multiple instances of evidence.

## Quick Reference: Spotting Safety Behaviours

‚ùì **Ask yourself:**
- Am I doing this to genuinely solve a problem, or to feel less anxious?
- Would I still do this if I wasn't anxious?
- Does this behaviour stop me from learning I'd be OK?
- Am I relying on this more over time, not less?
- What would confident-me do differently?

If the answer points to anxiety-reduction rather than problem-solving, it's likely a safety behaviour worth examining.
